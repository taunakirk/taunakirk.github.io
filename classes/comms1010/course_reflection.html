Communications 1010 GE Signature Assignment

Based on the assessments, what are your strengths as a communicator?

My strengths as a communicator include public speaking, participating in group discussions, and communicating in meetings. I used to be in a church when I lived in North Idaho. My job in the church was to be an evangelist, I was to meet random people on campus and engage in conversation and eventually invite them to a house gathering we hosted every week. I had to learn how to communicate effectively and also listen effectively. It was very hard for me to get to the point of expressing what I believed in and the confidence to say it to strangers even with the possibility of judgment. I did end up leaving the religion but I really appreciate the communication skills I gained while I was in it. I learned to talk to someone and listen to them respond back and find common ground. After talking to someone and engaging in friendship, those people always gave feedback that they were more open to talk to me based on how I engaged in conversation instead of just feeding them information.

I took that skill into my workplace. Whether I am in meetings or having a discussion with an engineer, I always found that the target of the conversation is finding common ground as we were trying to solve a problem. It didn’t help if we had different ideas and were strung on those ideas but that we would compromise on our ideas. Everytime we communicated like this at work we found that our work relationships grew and the confidence to express our thoughts and ideas became second nature to us.

Based on the assessments, what are your weaknesses? What can you do to start improving your communication competence now?

I found that my weaknesses were speeches. I can talk well in a public setting when there is a group and they engage in the speech I am giving. The weakness was if I am giving a speech with no feedback or input on the topic I was talking about. I find that I get nervous depending on how nervous I am showing. It is not the speech itself I am nervous about but how others perceive how nervous I look. I believe this is a root insecurity of mine based on past trauma. I am more worried about how others perceive me and then I try really hard to not look like that. It tends to make me look even more nervous or stumble on my speech because I am focusing more on how I look and not the speech itself.

I think to improve on this is to not think about what others think of me. I am the one giving the speech, I should be focused on the words I am going to say. I believe it is a major mind game I play to myself. I need to be able to change the thoughts I have to confidence in what I am saying. I am not one hundred percent on how to improve this, I am in therapy working on it so I hope that it will help me cope in the moments of me giving a speech.

What do you think has most impacted your perception of others? Think about your culture, family, and social influences. Based on what you have learned about perception of others, choose one concept you plan to improve on moving forward? (impression management; cognitive complexity; attribution error; etc from Chapter 2.2)

The impact of my perception of others is always thinking of the innocence or the positive of that individual. I grew up in a household where my parents were verbally judging people in their lives or strangers. I always felt negative in those moments and I really did not like it. As someone who has gone through a lot of trauma and hardships I tend to see the positive in people or rather hope to see the positive of them. Sometimes I perceive someone who has good intentions and get into a situation where my safety is at risk. This is the “Halo Effect”. A nice smile from a stranger and I think, “Oh they must be a kind person.” It allows me to not feel on edge all the time but also brings naivety into my head.

I have been in situations where I would be walking home at night and see a man walking with a pack and no shoes and I perceive them as someone who is having a hard time and I feel empathy for them. They will then “cat call” me or be really suspicious to me and then I have to go to survival mode quickly. I plan on improving having a neutral perception of others specifically in already risky situations.

What do you think has most impacted your perception of self and/or self-presentation? Think about your culture, family, and social influences. Based on what you have learned about perception of self, choose one concept you plan to improve on moving forward? (ethical communication; influences on self perception; self-presentation; self-efficacy; etc. from Chapter 2.3)

The thing that most impacted on my perception of self is specifically the trauma I endured growing up in my family and the church I used to attend. I grew up where I was the “bad” child. Words or threats like “if you keep acting like this we will have to send you away” or “look at your sister, she listens and is a good girl.” Going through therapy I learned these were lies or threats because my parents didn’t want to fully understand me or the reasons for my actions. I wish I could say my perception of self changed for the positive but it continues to be negative. I try very hard on first impressions or in relationships to be the most kind, non-self- serving, or the most positive person. I have found this to be very tiring as sometimes I don't feel good mentally or physically and I lie to myself to make sure and preserve the positive side people perceive of me.

I want to improve on this. I want to come off as natural or honest to others. I want to perceive how the other party is interacting and match what they are communicating. Instead of just making sure they get a perfect perception of me, I want them to honestly be able to perceive me based on the communication between us with honesty.

As you know, communication is irreversible and it shapes human identity and society. Knowing this, how can you adapt verbal and nonverbal messages for effectiveness while also not crossing the ethical line? How does gender, diversity, power, privilege, race, etc. impact this question of ethics? Could one person cross the line, but not another in the same situation? Use these questions to reflect on verbal and nonverbal messages and their ethical usage in your response.

I think some good ways of communicating effectively could be eye contact. When communicating eye contact is very important and tells the other party that you are listening to what they are saying with nonverbal cues. I think also mirroring body language shows the communicator that the listener is paying attention to them and this allows the communicator to feel comfortable. I think it's important to recognize that the ethics of communication is not practiced in a vacuum but filtered through social hierarchies. Those with higher social or organizational power have a greater burden to mind their “volume”. For example, a casual suggestion by a CEO could feel like a command to an intern. Ethical communication requires active-space making.

When it comes to gender and race the factors of ethical communication influences how messages are perceived regardless of their intent. When a man verbally communicates with an assertive tone it is often rewarded but when a woman uses assertive communication she is labeled as “aggressive”. To be ethical, I must acknowledge the biases. An ethical communicator recognizes their “blind spots” and avoids using microaggressions that reinforce the systemic inequalities. I do believe that one person can cross the line but not another in the same situation. An example would be if a person is using a term of endearment specific to their own racial or cultural community, they are participating in identity-building and solidarity. You take another person who is an outsider of that community and they use the same term they are seen as engaging in cultural appropriation or mockery. This is not a double standard but it's a recognition that identity grants or denies permission for certain types of communication.
